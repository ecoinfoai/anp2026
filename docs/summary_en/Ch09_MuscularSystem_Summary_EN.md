# Chapter 09. Muscular System

## 1. Skeletal Muscle

### Types of Muscle
- **Three types**: skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, smooth muscle
- **Skeletal muscle (striated muscle, voluntary muscle)**: approximately 650 muscles; comprises 40-45% of body weight
- **Cardiac muscle**: striated but involuntary
- **Smooth muscle**: no striations; involuntary; controls visceral movements

### Structure of Muscle
- **Muscle fiber** (contractile fibrous cell): diameter 50-100 μm; cylindrical shape
- **Sarcolemma** (muscle cell membrane): thickness ~100 Å; exhibits excitability and conductivity
- **Myofibril** (contractile protein unit): diameter ~1 μm
- **Sarcomere** (basic contractile unit): located between Z lines; length ~2.5 μm
- **Band structure**:
  - A band (dark band, anisotropic)
  - I band (light band, isotropic)
  - H zone
  - Z line
- **Myofilaments**:
  - Thick filament (myosin): diameter ~100 Å; located in A band
  - Thin filament (actin): diameter ~50 Å; extends from I band into A band
- **Sliding Theory** (Huxley, 1954): A band remains unchanged; I band shortens during contraction
- **Muscle fiber types**:
  - White muscle (fast twitch): more myofibrils; fast and strong contraction; fatigues easily; for rapid movements
  - Red muscle (slow twitch): fewer myofibrils; slow contraction; fatigue-resistant; maintains posture

### Nerve Supply to Muscles
- **Somatic motor fiber**: transmits contraction commands via motor endplate
- **Neurotransmitter**: acetylcholine secretion triggers muscle contraction
- **Motor unit** (neuromuscular unit): muscle fibers innervated by one motor neuron; ranges from few fibers to ~150 fibers
- **Somatic sensory fibers** (three types):
  - Free nerve endings: transmit pain
  - Muscle spindle (neuromuscular spindle): regulates muscle contraction and tone
  - Golgi tendon organ (GTO) (neurotendinous organ): deep sensory fiber for equilibrium
- **Autonomic nerve supply**: sympathetic fibers (along arteries); parasympathetic fibers (with motor and sensory nerves)

### Skeletal Muscle Contraction
- **Contraction mechanism**: thin and thick filaments slide past each other → Z lines shorten
- **Regulatory proteins**: tropomyosin and troponin complex block filament interaction
- **Role of calcium (Ca²⁺)**:
  - Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) releases Ca²⁺
  - Ca²⁺ binds to troponin C → tropomyosin shifts → myosin head binds to actin
- **Troponin subunits**: troponin T (TnT); troponin C (TnC); troponin I (TnI)
- **Energy source**: adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
- **ATP regeneration** (three methods):
  - Aerobic metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation)
  - Anaerobic metabolism (lactic acid pathway)
  - Creatine phosphate (CP) system
- **Action potential and muscle response**:
  - Depolarization → Ca²⁺ release → contraction
  - Repolarization → Ca²⁺ reuptake → relaxation

### Muscle Attachment and Action
- **Origin**: fixed point of muscle attachment; located closer to midline
- **Insertion**: mobile point of muscle attachment; located farther from midline
- **Primary mover** (agonist): main muscle driving the movement
- **Assistant muscle** (secondary mover): aids primary mover
- **Synergist**: contracts simultaneously to coordinate movement
- **Antagonist**: opposes movement direction

### Neuromuscular Junction
- **Neuromuscular junction** (myoneural junction): contact point between motor nerve terminal and muscle cell membrane
- **Sequence**: nerve excitation → acetylcholine secretion → endplate potential → muscle action potential → contraction
- **Muscle tonus** (muscle tone): constant light tetanic contraction; maintained by continuous motor input; lost during anesthesia or sleep

### Electromyogram (EMG)
- Records electrical potentials (action potentials) during muscle contraction; duration approximately 1/1000 second
- Observed using oscilloscope
- Clinical utility: diagnoses nerve and muscle diseases

### Types of Muscle Contraction

#### Twitch Contraction
- Single stimulus triggers single contraction
- Latency period: 0.01-0.02 seconds
- Duration: approximately 0.1 seconds
- Observed in tendon reflexes

#### Tetanic Contraction
- Repeated stimuli produce fused contractions
- Results in 50% muscle shortening
- Represents most in-vivo skeletal muscle contractions

#### Contracture
- Sustained contraction following vigorous exercise
- Reversible condition
- No action potential present
- Due to sustained depolarization

#### Rigor Mortis
- Post-mortem muscle rigidity begins 1-2 hours after death
- Progresses from superior to inferior (top to bottom)
- Caused by adenosine triphosphate (ATP) depletion
- Irreversible condition

#### Tonus (Muscle Tone)
- Light tonic contraction of muscle fibers
- Maintains posture
- Requires minimal energy expenditure

#### Contraction Types Based on Muscle Length
- **Isotonic contraction**: muscle length changes; includes:
  - Concentric contraction: muscle shortens
  - Eccentric contraction: muscle lengthens while contracting
- **Isometric contraction**: no change in muscle length; static contraction (e.g., plank hold, dead hang)
- **Isokinetic contraction**: constant speed of contraction; used in rehabilitation and physical therapy

## 2. Muscles of Body Parts

### Muscles of the Head

#### Facial Muscles
Innervated by facial nerve (CN VII):
- **Occipitofrontalis**: composed of frontalis and occipitalis
  - Frontalis: raises eyebrows
  - Occipitalis: causes scalp to wrinkle
- **Orbicularis oculi**: closes eyelids; produces winking
- **Nasalis**: widens nostrils
- **Buccinator**: compresses cheek; holds food in mouth; assists sucking
- **Orbicularis oris**: closes mouth; produces whistling
- **Risorius**: creates dimple in cheek
- **Zygomaticus major**: produces smiling
- **Zygomaticus minor**: raises upper lip; expression of disdain
- **Levator labii superioris**: facial expression (dislike)
- **Depressor anguli oris**: produces sad expression
- **Depressor labii inferioris**: causes lower lip to pout

#### Masticatory Muscles
Innervated by mandibular division of trigeminal nerve (CN V₃); four pairs:
- **Masseter**: extends from zygomatic arch to mandibular angle
- **Temporalis**: extends from temporal fossa to coronoid process
- **Lateral pterygoid**: assists in jaw opening
- **Medial pterygoid**: produces rotational movement during chewing

#### Ocular Muscles
Six extraocular muscles plus levator palpebrae superioris (7 total):
- **Oculomotor nerve (CN III)**: innervates superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, and inferior oblique muscles
- **Abducens nerve (CN VI)**: innervates lateral rectus
- **Trochlear nerve (CN IV)**: innervates superior oblique

### Muscles of the Neck

#### Suprahyoid Muscles
Four pairs elevate hyoid bone and tongue:
- Digastric
- Mylohyoid
- Stylohyoid
- Geniohyoid

#### Infrahyoid Muscles
Four pairs depress hyoid bone and pharynx:
- Omohyoid
- Sternohyoid
- Sternothyroid
- Thyrohyoid

#### Other Neck Muscles
- **Platysma**: innervated by facial nerve (CN VII); pulls mouth corner downward
- **Sternocleidomastoid (SCM)** (sternomastoid muscle): innervated by accessory nerve (CN XI) and cervical plexus
  - Unilateral contraction: rotates head to opposite side
  - Bilateral contraction: raises face (extends neck)

### Muscles of the Back

#### Superficial Layer
- **Trapezius**: adducts scapula
- **Latissimus dorsi**: adducts and medially rotates arm
- **Rhomboid**: stabilizes scapula
- **Levator scapulae**: elevates scapula

#### Intermediate Layer
- **Serratus posterior superior**: elevates rib cage
- **Serratus posterior inferior**: depresses rib cage

#### Deep Layer
- **Erector spinae** (sacrospinalis group): extends vertebral column
  - Iliocostalis: lateral column
  - Longissimus: intermediate column
  - Spinalis: medial column

### Muscles of the Chest

#### Superficial Muscles
- **Pectoralis major**: adducts and medially rotates arm; assists inspiration
- **Pectoralis minor**: stabilizes and depresses scapula
- **Serratus anterior** (boxer's muscle): protacts and elevates scapula

#### Intercostal Muscles
- **External intercostals**: elevate rib cage; assist inspiration
- **Internal intercostals**: depress rib cage; assist expiration
- **Transversus thoracis**: assists expiration

#### Diaphragm
- **Function**: primary muscle of respiration
  - Contraction: produces inspiration
  - Relaxation: produces expiration
- **Innervation**: phrenic nerve
- **Three major openings** (from superior to inferior):
  - Aortic hiatus: passage for descending thoracic aorta
  - Vena cava hiatus: passage for inferior vena cava
  - Esophageal hiatus: passage for esophagus
- **Respiration types**:
  - Abdominal respiration: primarily diaphragmatic contraction
  - Thoracic respiration: primarily intercostal muscle contraction

### Muscles of the Abdomen

#### Anterior Wall
- **Rectus abdominis**: 3-4 tendinous intersections; bounded by linea alba (medially) and linea semilunaris (laterally)

#### Lateral Wall
- **External oblique**: lateral and anterior rotation of trunk
- **Internal oblique** (contains cremaster reflex): lateral and anterior rotation of trunk
- **Transverse abdominis**: compresses abdominal contents

#### Posterior Wall
- **Quadratus lumborum**: lateral flexion of vertebral column

#### Associated Structures
- **Inguinal ligament**: connects anterior superior iliac spine to pubic tubercle
- **Inguinal canal**: passage in lower abdominal wall
- **Inguinal hernia**: protrusion of abdominal contents through inguinal canal

### Muscles of the Upper Limb

#### Rotator Cuff Muscles (SITS)
Stabilize glenohumeral joint:
- **S - Supraspinatus**: initiates arm abduction
- **I - Infraspinatus**: externally rotates arm
- **T - Teres minor**: externally rotates arm
- **S - Subscapularis**: internally rotates arm

#### Shoulder Muscles
- **Deltoid**: produces arm flexion, extension, and abduction; landmark for intramuscular injection

#### Arm Muscles
- **Biceps brachii**: produces elbow flexion and supination
- **Triceps brachii**: produces forearm extension; innervated by radial nerve
- **Brachialis**: produces forearm flexion

#### Forearm Muscles
Total of 19 muscles (8 anterior flexors; 11 posterior extensors):
- **Flexor retinaculum**: anterior wrist structure
- **Extensor retinaculum**: posterior wrist structure

#### Intrinsic Hand Muscles
- Thenar group: controls thumb movement
- Midpalmar group: controls finger movement
- Hypothenar group: controls little finger movement

### Muscles of the Lower Limb

#### Gluteal Region

**Gluteus maximus**:
- Primary hip extensor
- Produces lateral rotation of thigh
- Innervated by inferior gluteal nerve

**Gluteus medius**:
- Produces thigh abduction
- Produces medial rotation of thigh
- Innervated by superior gluteal nerve

**Gluteus minimus**:
- Produces thigh abduction
- Produces medial rotation of thigh
- Innervated by superior gluteal nerve

**Iliopsoas**:
- Produces thigh flexion

#### Anterior Thigh

**Sartorius**:
- Longest muscle in human body
- Also called tailor's muscle
- Produces hip flexion and knee flexion

**Quadriceps femoris** (largest muscle of lower limb):
- Produces knee extension
- Four components:
  - Rectus femoris
  - Vastus medialis
  - Vastus intermedius
  - Vastus lateralis

#### Posterior Thigh (Hamstring Muscles)

**Three hamstring muscles**:
- **Biceps femoris**: produces knee flexion and hip extension
- **Semitendinosus**: produces knee flexion and hip extension
- **Semimembranosus**: produces knee flexion and hip extension
- **Innervation**: sciatic nerve
- **Clinical significance**: tripod muscle involved in standing and walking

#### Anterior Leg

- **Tibialis anterior**: dorsiflexion of foot; innervated by deep peroneal nerve (deep fibular nerve)
- **Extensor digitorum longus**: extends toes; innervated by deep peroneal nerve
- **Extensor hallucis longus**: extends great toe; innervated by deep peroneal nerve

#### Posterior Leg

**Triceps surae**:
- Composed of gastrocnemius and soleus
- Connected to calcaneal bone via calcaneal tendon (Achilles tendon)
- Produces plantar flexion
- Innervated by tibial nerve

#### Intrinsic Foot Muscles

- **Dorsal muscles**: extensor digitorum brevis extends toes
- **Plantar muscles**: four layers control fine foot movements and maintain arch

## Summary

The muscular system comprises skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle tissues. This chapter focused on skeletal muscle structure, physiology, and the major muscle groups of the body. Understanding muscle anatomy, innervation, and function is essential for clinical assessment, rehabilitation, and understanding human movement patterns.
