Chapter 10. Endocrine System

1. Pituitary Gland

Overview of the Endocrine System

  • Definition: The endocrine system produces hormones that are released directly into the circulatory system to regulate metabolism and bodily processes
  • Endocrine glands: Thyroid, pituitary, adrenal, pancreas, thymus, parathyroid, testis (female: ovary)
  • Hormone: Derived from the Greek word meaning "substance that awakens from sleep"
  • Biological characteristics of hormones:
    • Highly effective even in very small amounts
    • No species specificity; effects are the same across animal species
    • Often do not produce antibodies when injected into different species
    • Relatively simple structure and small molecular weight even as proteins
    • Hormones other than sex hormones and thyroid hormones are broken down by digestive enzymes when administered orally
    • Both excess and deficient secretion is harmful; daily secretion depends on body weight, age, sex, and physical activity

Pituitary Structure (Pituitary body, Hypophysis)

  • Location: Within the sella turcica of the sphenoid bone at the base of the cranial cavity
  • Size: Pea-sized; male 0.5–0.6g, female 0.6–0.7g (female slightly heavier)
  • Connection: Connected to the floor of the third ventricle via the infundibulum
  • Composition:
    • Adenohypophysis → anterior lobe + pars intermedia
    • Neurohypophysis → posterior lobe + infundibulum

Anterior Lobe Hormones

  • TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone): Promotes thyroid development, stimulates thyroxine (T₄) secretion, negative feedback mechanism
  • ACTH (Adrenocorticotropic Hormone): 33-amino acid polypeptide, regulates adrenal cortex endocrine activity, promotes glucocorticoid secretion, negative feedback mechanism
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Female — promotes ovarian cell activity/maturation, stimulates estrogen secretion / Male — acts on Sertoli's cells (supporting cells) to promote spermatogenesis
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Female — promotes ovulation, corpus luteum formation, progesterone secretion / Male — acts on Leydig's cells (interstitial cells) to promote testosterone secretion → also called ICSH (Interstitial Cell Stimulating Hormone)
  • GH (Growth Hormone): Promotes bone and soft tissue growth, stimulates gluconeogenesis and glycogen breakdown, raises blood fat levels. Also called somatotropin (STH). Excess → gigantism; excess after growth → acromegaly; deficiency during growth → dwarfism
  • Prolactin (PRL): Targets only the mammary gland, molecular weight ~25,000, stimulates milk secretion. Also called luteotropic hormone (LTH)

Posterior Lobe Hormones

  • ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone, Vasopressin): Promotes water reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct, inhibits diuresis. Deficiency → diabetes insipidus (excessive urination, severe thirst)
  • Oxytocin (OT): Contracts uterine muscles → promotes labor; contracts myoepithelial cells on alveolar walls of mammary glands → milk ejection

2. Thyroid Gland

Thyroid Structure

  • Weight: ~30g, the largest endocrine gland in humans
  • Location: Anterior neck, superior to the trachea
  • Shape: Butterfly-like appearance
  • Composition: Two lateral lobes (left and right) + isthmus
  • Histological features: Follicular structure, high iodine content
  • Hormones: Primarily thyroxine (T₄), some triiodothyronine (T₃). Same function but T₃ is more potent. Parafollicular cells also secrete thyrocalcitonin

Thyroid Hormone Synthesis and Secretion

  • Dietary iodine → absorbed in GI tract → transported via blood → thyroid follicular cells actively take up iodine
  • Synthesized thyroid hormone stored in colloid bound to protein
  • Released by separating from protein into the bloodstream
  • Regulated by TSH from the anterior pituitary

Physiological Actions of Thyroid Hormones

  • Promotion of metabolism
  • Growth and development (especially bones, teeth, skeletal muscles)
  • Enhancement of sympathetic nervous activity → increased heart rate, vasodilation, increased blood flow, sweating
  • Promotion of intellectual development and mental function
  • Stimulation of milk secretion from mammary glands
  • Increased blood sugar through sugar absorption and gluconeogenesis promotion
  • Promotion of cholesterol excretion via bile

Hypothyroidism

  • Children: Cretinism — dwarfism, impaired CNS development, delayed cognitive development
  • Adults: Myxedema — mental sluggishness, slow heartbeat, low skin temperature, dry skin, hair loss, subcutaneous mucoid substance accumulation → edema

Hyperthyroidism

  • Basedow's disease = Graves' disease
  • Three symptoms: Exophthalmos, tachycardia, goiter
  • Elevated basal metabolism, increased appetite, weight loss
  • Treatment: Partial thyroidectomy or radioactive iodine (I¹³¹)

Thyrocalcitonin

  • Also called calcitonin (CT)
  • Lowers blood calcium concentration → hypocalcemic hormone
  • Antagonistic action against parathormone (hypercalcemic hormone)
  • Inhibits osteoclast activity → suppresses Ca²⁺ release from bone
  • Increases osteocyte activity → Ca²⁺ deposition → lowered blood Ca²⁺

3. Parathyroid Gland

Parathyroid Structure

  • Location: Posterior surface of thyroid, one pair above and one pair below, 4 total
  • Size: Rice grain–sized
  • Hormone: Parathormone (PTH)

Parathormone Function

  • Hypercalcemic hormone — raises blood Ca²⁺ concentration
  • Involved in bone and tooth development
  • PTH decrease → blood calcium falls to ~1/2 of normal → muscle spasm → tetany (more common in children and pregnant women)
  • Ca²⁺ ion regulation: decreased Ca²⁺ → stimulates parathyroid → PTH secretion → releases Ca²⁺ from bone into body fluid
  • Blood Ca²⁺ regulation: Coordinated action of parathormone, calcitonin, and calcitriol

4. Pancreas

Pancreas Structure

  • Location: Behind the stomach, anterior to L1–L2 vertebrae, horizontal across posterior abdominal wall
  • Parts: Head, body, tail
  • Function: Both exocrine (digestive gland) and endocrine (hormones)
  • Pancreatic islets (Islets of Langerhans): ~1 million, most abundant in the tail, 1–2% of total pancreatic weight
    • α cells (10–30%): Stained red by aniline, secrete glucagon
    • β cells (60–90%): Stained blue-violet by aniline, secrete insulin
    • δ cells (1–8%): No granules, secrete somatostatin

Insulin

  • Protein hormone secreted by β cells
  • Physiological actions:
    • Lowers blood glucose: facilitates glucose entry into tissue cells
    • Hyperpolarization: increases resting membrane potential, raises intracellular K⁺ and lowers extracellular K⁺
    • Promotes conversion of sugar to fat
    • Promotes protein synthesis

Diabetes Mellitus

  • Caused by hyperglycemia due to insulin deficiency
  • Type I (IDDM): Insulin-dependent, β cell destruction, mainly in children, requires insulin treatment
  • Type II (NIDDM): Non-insulin-dependent, decreased cellular responsiveness to insulin, reduced tissue sensitivity
  • Symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia, weight loss, decreased libido

Glucagon

  • Secreted by α cells, 29-amino acid polypeptide
  • Raises blood glucose, antagonistic to insulin
  • Main target organ: liver
  • Neither excess nor deficiency causes significant disorders

5. Adrenal Gland

Adrenal Structure

  • Location: One pair, attached above each kidney
  • Cortex: Yellow, relatively firm tissue
  • Medulla: Dark brown, relatively soft, chromaffin cells

Adrenal Medulla Hormones

  • Epinephrine (adrenaline): Predominantly secreted in humans and dogs
  • Norepinephrine (noradrenaline): Precursor of epinephrine, neurotransmitter at postganglionic fiber terminals. Predominantly secreted in cats
  • Catecholamines: Epinephrine + norepinephrine + dopamine. Biosynthesized from phenylalanine. Methylation of norepinephrine → epinephrine
  • Epinephrine effects: Strong cardiac stimulation, skeletal muscle vasodilation, bronchodilation, glycogenolysis → blood glucose increase
  • Norepinephrine effects: Weaker cardiac stimulation, vasoconstriction → significant blood pressure increase, arteriole constriction in skeletal muscle, skin, and viscera

Adrenal Cortex Hormones

  • Hyperfunction → Cushing syndrome; hypofunction → Addison's disease
  • Zona glomerulosa: Outermost layer, secretes mineralocorticoids → aldosterone — promotes Na⁺ reabsorption, regulates body fluid
  • Zona fasciculata: Middle layer, thickest (>⅔ of cortex), secretes glucocorticoids → cortisol, cortisone
  • Zona reticularis: Innermost layer, secretes androgens + small amount of glucocorticoids

Major Actions of Glucocorticoids

  • Conversion of fat and protein to carbohydrates → gluconeogenesis
  • Anti-inflammatory action → treatment of rheumatic diseases
  • Decrease lymphocytes, eosinophils, basophils / increase neutrophils, RBCs, platelets
  • Promote gastric acid and pepsin secretion, inhibit mucus secretion → reduce gastric mucosal resistance
  • Increase blood glucose → promote normal cellular metabolism
  • Inhibit prostaglandins → reduce pain
  • Stress response: maintain internal environment homeostasis

6. Gastrointestinal Hormones

Overview

  • Hormones that promote or inhibit digestive tract activity (digestive secretion and motility)
  • Secreted by specialized cells of the GI mucosa → gastrointestinal hormones (GI hormones)

Secretin

  • Secreted from the duodenal mucosa
  • Stimulates pancreatic juice secretion rich in HCO₃⁻ ions (low in digestive enzymes)

Gastrin

  • Secreted by G cells in the pyloric mucosa of the stomach
  • Stimulates gastric juice secretion and gastric motility

Cholecystokinin-Pancreozymin (CCK-PZ)

  • Secreted when food enters the duodenum
  • CCK: Contracts the gallbladder
  • PZ: Stimulates pancreatic juice rich in digestive enzymes and low in HCO⁻ ions

Other GI Hormones

  • GIP (Gastric Inhibitory Peptide): Inhibits gastric HCl secretion, promotes pancreatic insulin secretion
  • Motilin: Promotes gastric and small intestinal motility
  • VIP (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide): Inhibits GI motility, lowers blood pressure

7. Hormone-Related Substances

Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP)

  • Opposite to angiotensin-II: promotes water and sodium ion excretion from kidneys
  • Inhibits ADH and aldosterone secretion → decreases blood volume and pressure

Calcitriol

  • Steroid hormone secreted in response to parathormone stimulation
  • Promotes calcium and phosphorus absorption in the intestinal tract

Erythropoietin (EPO)

  • Peptide hormone secreted when renal cells are oxygen-deficient
  • Stimulates red bone marrow → promotes erythropoiesis

Renin

  • Secreted when blood pressure drops
  • Stimulates the angiotensin system → induces blood pressure elevation

Melatonin

  • Secreted by the pineal body
  • Related to light sensation, inhibits reproductive organ development
  • Blood concentration: 10 times higher at night than during the day

Prostaglandin (PG)

  • Multiple types (A, B, C, D, E, F, G)
  • Vasodilation, smooth muscle contraction or relaxation (especially uterine and bronchial muscles)
  • Promotes or inhibits platelet aggregation, corpus luteum regression, uterine contraction

Inhibin

  • Glycoprotein secreted by Sertoli cells (testis) and granulosa cells (ovary)
  • Inhibin A and Inhibin B
  • Acts on pituitary to inhibit FSH secretion

Leptin

  • Protein hormone secreted by adipose tissue, acts on hypothalamus
  • Provides satiety, secretion begins 20 minutes after eating
  • Higher blood leptin levels in obese individuals
