Chapter 13. Nervous System

1. Neuroanatomy and Neurophysiology

Organization of the Nervous System

  • Definition: The nervous system is unique to animals, receiving stimuli via afferent nerve fibers to the CNS for integration, then sending impulses via efferent nerve fibers to effectors for adaptation to environmental changes.
  • Structural classification:
    • Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain (in cranial cavity) and spinal cord (in vertebral canal). Serves as integrative and control center
    • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): 12 pairs of cranial nerves and 31 pairs of spinal nerves
  • Functional classification:
    • Afferent division: Somatic sensory nerve cells and visceral sensory nerve cells
    • Efferent division: Somatic nervous system (SNS, voluntary) and Autonomic nervous system (ANS, involuntary)
    • ANS: Sympathetic division and parasympathetic division

Components of the Nervous System

  • Neurons (nerve cells): Functional unit of nervous tissue. Cell body and processes
    • Axon: Conducts impulses from cell body to periphery. Typically one per neuron, can reach 1m in length
    • Dendrite: Transmits sensory impulses to cell body. One or multiple
    • Nissl's body: Protein synthesis site, rich in ribosomes, stains with methylene blue
    • Neurofibril: Fine fibers (~100Å), supportive role in cytoplasm and processes
  • Neuron size: 4–100㎛
  • Structural classification: Unipolar, bipolar, multipolar, anaxonic neurons
  • Functional classification: Sensory neurons, interneurons, motor neurons
  • Neuroglia: Support, debris cleanup, material supply, myelin formation, phagocytosis
    • CNS: Ependymal cells (CSF secretion), astrocytes (metabolism), oligodendrocytes (myelin formation), microglia (phagocytosis)
    • PNS: Schwann cells (myelin formation/maintenance), satellite cells (neuron body protection)

Nerve Fibers and Myelin

  • Nerve fiber: Axon covered by sheath. Central axon surrounded by myelin sheath
  • Myelin sheath: Composed of myelin (lipoid substance), acts as electrical insulator
  • Node of Ranvier: Gaps in myelin sheath at 0.1–1.0mm intervals
  • Myelinated fibers: Fast conduction / Unmyelinated fibers: Slow conduction
  • Schwann's sheath (neurilemma): Present only in peripheral nerve fibers, absent in CNS

Synapse

  • Definition: Junction between neurons. Axon terminal forms terminal bouton contacting next neuron
  • Synaptic cleft: 100–200Å gap
  • Synaptic vesicles: 300–600Å diameter, present only on presynaptic side → unidirectional transmission
  • Electrical synapse: Gap junction, 3.5nm gap, bidirectional, no synaptic delay
  • Chemical synapse: Neurotransmitter-mediated, 30–50nm gap, unidirectional, synaptic delay 0.5–1m/s

Nerve Impulse Conduction

  • Resting membrane potential: –70mV in neurons
  • Na⁺-K⁺ pump: Transports 2 K⁺ in and 3 Na⁺ out per cycle
  • K⁺ concentration: ~30 times higher inside cell; Na⁺ concentration: ~10 times higher outside
  • Equilibrium potential: ENa⁺ = +65mV, EK⁺ = –97mV → resting potential approximates K⁺ equilibrium
  • Action potential: Threshold (–55mV) → depolarization → Na⁺ influx → +30mV (overshoot) → K⁺ efflux → repolarization → hyperpolarization → recovery
  • Refractory period: 0.001–0.005 sec. Absolute (Na⁺ permeability phase) and relative (K⁺ permeability phase)
  • All-or-none law: Above-threshold stimulus produces full action potential; below-threshold produces none
  • Saltatory conduction: In myelinated fibers, impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier. Increases speed, conserves energy

2. Central Nervous System

Cerebral Hemispheres

  • 80% of total brain weight. Separated by longitudinal fissure
  • 5 lobes: Frontal, parietal, occipital, temporal, insular
  • Cerebral cortex: ~3mm deep gray matter, 6 layers, area 2,000–2,500cm²
  • Brodmann: Divided cortex into 52 areas. Functional localization

Major Cortical Areas

  • Primary somatomotor area (Brodmann area 4): Precentral gyrus. Pyramidal motor system. Body representation proportional to use/fine movement. Lower body → upper cortex
  • Premotor area (Brodmann area 6): Extrapyramidal motor system
  • Motor speech area (Broca's area, Brodmann 44): In dominant hemisphere. Damage → motor aphasia
  • Prefrontal area (Brodmann 9, 10, 11): Judgment, thought, personality, insight
  • Primary somatosensory area (Brodmann 1, 2, 3): Postcentral gyrus. Proportional to receptor density
  • Auditory association area (Brodmann 22, Wernicke's area): Damage → sensory aphasia
  • Primary visual area (Brodmann 17): Occipital lobe
  • Primary taste area (Brodmann 43): Base of postcentral gyrus in parietal lobe

Cerebral Medulla and Basal Ganglia

  • Projection fibers: Corona radiata, internal capsule
  • Association fibers: Connect cortical areas within same hemisphere (short and long)
  • Commissural fibers: Connect opposite hemispheres. Corpus callosum is the largest
  • Basal ganglia: 4 pairs — caudate nucleus, lentiform nucleus (globus pallidus + putamen), claustrum, amygdaloid body
    • Parkinson's disease: Substantia nigra damage → dopamine deficiency
    • Huntington's chorea: GABA-secreting neuron disorder, hereditary
    • Athetosis: Lentiform nucleus damage
    • Hemiballismus: Contralateral subthalamic nucleus damage

Limbic System and Brain Waves

  • Limbic system: Autonomic functions, self-preservation instincts, primitive sensation
  • EEG waves: Alpha (8–12cps, relaxed), beta (14–30cps, aroused), delta (1–4cps, sleep), theta (4–7cps, emotional/long-term memory)

Sleep

  • Non-REM sleep: Stage 1 (theta waves), Stage 2 (sleep spindles, K-complex), Stages 3-4 (delta waves, slow-wave sleep). No dreams, parasympathetic dominance
  • REM sleep (paradoxical sleep): Rapid eye movement, skeletal muscle atonia, 80% dreaming

Diencephalon

  • Thalamus: Relay station for all senses except olfaction
  • Epithalamus: Pineal body — melatonin secretion, sexual function, biorhythm
  • Metathalamus: Medial geniculate body (auditory relay), lateral geniculate body (visual relay)
  • Hypothalamus: Highest autonomic center. Temperature regulation, food intake (satiety/feeding centers), thirst, emotion, sexual behavior, cardiovascular/digestive regulation, pituitary control

Brain Stem

  • Medulla oblongata: Lowest part. Respiratory, cardiac, vasomotor centers. Pyramidal decussation
  • Pons: Cranial nerves V, VI, VII, VIII originate here. Rhomboid fossa
  • Midbrain: Superior colliculus (visual reflex), inferior colliculus (auditory relay), red nucleus (extrapyramidal), substantia nigra
  • Reticular formation: Receives sensory, motor, vestibular, proprioceptive information. Ascending reticular activating system → arousal

Cerebellum

  • Weight 120–150g, 1/8 of cerebrum. Balance, fine motor coordination
  • Cerebellar cortex: 3 layers (molecular, Purkinje cell, granular), 0.5–0.7mm thick
  • Cerebellar nuclei: Dentate (voluntary movement control), emboliform (gravity support), globose (muscle tone), fastigial (equilibrium/rotation)

Spinal Cord

  • ~1cm diameter, 40–45cm length, 45g weight. Ends at L1–L2 as medullary cone
  • Gray matter: H-shaped. Anterior horn (motor), posterior horn (sensory), lateral horn (autonomic)
  • White matter: Anterior, lateral, posterior funiculi
  • Spinal nerves: 31 pairs (cervical 8, thoracic 12, lumbar 5, sacral 5, coccygeal 1)
  • Ascending tracts: Posterior column, spinocerebellar, spinothalamic
  • Descending tracts: Pyramidal (lateral corticospinal 75–80%, anterior corticospinal, corticobulbar); Extrapyramidal (rubrospinal, vestibulospinal, reticulospinal, tectospinal)
  • Reflexes: Stretch reflex, withdrawal reflex, crossed extension reflex, autonomic reflex

Ventricles and CSF

  • Ventricles: Lateral, third, cerebral aqueduct, fourth. Choroid plexus produces CSF
  • CSF: 130–150ml. Pressure: 130–150mmH₂O supine, 200–250mmH₂O sitting
  • Lumbar puncture: Between L3–L4

Meninges

  • Dura mater: Periosteal and meningeal layers. Falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli, diaphragma sellae, falx cerebelli
  • Arachnoid mater: Subarachnoid space contains CSF and blood vessels
  • Pia mater: Vascular, contributes to choroid plexus formation

3. Peripheral Nervous System

Cranial Nerves — 12 Pairs

  • I. Olfactory: Sensory. Shortest cranial nerve
  • II. Optic: Sensory. Visual conduction
  • III. Oculomotor: 4 eye muscles + levator palpebrae + parasympathetic (pupil constriction, ciliary muscle)
  • IV. Trochlear: Superior oblique muscle. Smallest cranial nerve
  • V. Trigeminal: Largest cranial nerve. 3 branches: ophthalmic, maxillary, mandibular. Facial sensation + mastication
  • VI. Abducens: Lateral rectus. Longest course among eye motor nerves
  • VII. Facial: Facial expression muscles, anterior 2/3 taste (chorda tympani), parasympathetic (sublingual, submandibular, lacrimal glands)
  • VIII. Vestibulocochlear: Hearing + equilibrium
  • IX. Glossopharyngeal: Posterior 1/3 taste, pharyngeal sensation/movement, parotid gland secretion
  • X. Vagus: Longest cranial nerve, widest distribution. Mainly parasympathetic. Recurrent laryngeal nerve damage → hoarseness
  • XI. Accessory: Pure motor. Sternocleidomastoid + trapezius
  • XII. Hypoglossal: Tongue movement (speech, chewing, swallowing)

Spinal Nerves — 31 Pairs

  • Bell-Magendie law: Anterior root = motor, posterior root = sensory
  • Nerve plexuses:
    • Cervical plexus (C₁–C₄): Phrenic nerve (C₃–C₅) — diaphragm innervation (most important)
    • Brachial plexus (C₅–T₁): Axillary, musculocutaneous, median, radial, ulnar nerves
    • Lumbar plexus (T₁₂–L₄): Femoral nerve, obturator nerve
    • Sacral plexus (L₄–S₄): Sciatic nerve (largest and longest nerve in body)

Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)

  • Sympathetic: Thoracolumbar division (C₈–L₃). Short preganglionic, long postganglionic fibers. Neurotransmitter: Norepinephrine (NE). "Fight or flight"
  • Parasympathetic: Craniosacral division (CN III, VII, IX, X + S₂–S₄). Long preganglionic, short postganglionic fibers. Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine (Ach). "Rest and relaxation"
  • Key comparisons: Sympathetic — increased heart rate, vasoconstriction, pupil dilation, digestive inhibition, bronchodilation / Parasympathetic — decreased heart rate, vasodilation, pupil constriction, digestive promotion, bronchoconstriction
