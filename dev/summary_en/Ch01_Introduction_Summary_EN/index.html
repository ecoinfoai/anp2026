Chapter 01. Introduction

1. Anatomy and Physiology

Overview

  • Physiology: A discipline that studies the functions, properties, and mechanisms of each organ in the living body, including the physical and chemical factors and processes within them.
  • Anatomy: A discipline that reveals the structure and form of the human body; it is morphology of the human body.

Anatomy

  • Etymology: A compound of Latin 'ana' and 'tomy', meaning 'cutting' or 'analysis' in English.
  • Human anatomy: The most fundamental discipline in medicine, providing knowledge about the structure, form, and position of organs and tissues in each body region.

History of Anatomy

  • Hippocrates (B.C. 460–377): First used the term 'anatomy'
  • Erasistratos (B.C. 292–201): First attempted human dissection
  • Galenus (130–201): Systematically dissected monkeys; his theory dominated until the 15th century
  • Andreas Vesalius (1514–1564): Published 『De Humani Corporis Fabrica』 in 1543; known as the 'Father of Modern Anatomy'

Classification of Anatomy

  • By research method:
    • Gross anatomy (macroscopic anatomy): Observes each body part with the naked eye using various instruments
    • Microscopic anatomy: Uses microscopes to observe and study the fine structure and form of cells and tissues
    • Embryology: Observes and studies the process from fertilized egg to growth of an individual
  • By research purpose:
    • Systemic anatomy: The most fundamental discipline of anatomy; observes human organs by dividing them into systematic groups
    • Pathological anatomy: A discipline for investigating the cause of disease or death
    • Forensic anatomy: A discipline for determining the cause of death through forensic anatomical investigation

Physiology

  • Etymology: Derived from Latin; 'physio' (nature) + 'logy' (science)
  • Definition: A discipline that studies the functions, properties, and mechanisms of living organisms
  • History:
    • Erasistratus (B.C. 292–201): Attempted to apply physical laws to the study of human body function; called the 'Father of Physiology'
    • Galenus (130–201): Recognized as the supreme authority in physiology until the Renaissance
    • William Harvey (1578–1657): Discovered the principle of blood circulation — that the heart pumps blood through closed vessels
    • Claude Bernard (1813–1878): 'Father of Modern Physiology'; stated that "the internal environment maintains a remarkable constancy despite changes in the external environment"
    • Walter Cannon (1871–1945): Named this constancy of the internal environment "homeostasis" in his book 『The Wisdom of the Body』
  • Classification: Plant physiology, animal physiology, bacterial physiology, virus physiology, cellular physiology, human physiology

2. Overview of the Human Body

Characteristics of Humans

  • Belong to mammals (mammalia), the most evolved among vertebrates.
  • Compared to other mammals, humans have very little body hair and a highly developed cerebrum.

Anatomical Terminology

Anatomical Position

  • Standing upright, eyes facing forward horizontally, arms hanging at the sides with palms facing forward, fingers extended straight

Body Planes

  • Midsagittal plane: Passes through the sagittal suture of the skull; divides the body equally into left and right halves
  • Sagittal plane: Parallel to the midsagittal plane; divides the body or organs into left and right portions
  • Frontal plane (coronal plane): Parallel to the forehead; divides the body or organs into anterior and posterior portions
  • Transverse plane (horizontal plane): Divides the body into superior and inferior portions; horizontal to the ground

Directional Terms

  • Anterior (ventral): Front side of the body or organ
  • Posterior (dorsal): Back side of the body or organ
  • Superior (cranial): Upper direction in standing position
  • Inferior (caudal): Lower direction in standing position
  • Medial: Closer to the midsagittal plane; ulnar (arm), tibial (leg)
  • Lateral: Farther from the midsagittal plane; radial (arm), fibular (leg)
  • Proximal: Closer to the center of the body
  • Distal: Farther from the center of the body
  • Parietal: Wall side of a body cavity
  • Visceral: Organ side of a body cavity
  • Superficial (external): Closer to the surface of the body or organ
  • Deep (internal): Deeper from the surface of the body or organ

Body Cavities

  • The human body has 5 body cavities.
  • Cranial cavity: Enclosed by the skull (cranium); contains the brain; communicates with the vertebral canal through the foramen magnum
  • Vertebral canal: Formed by the continuous alignment of vertebral foramina; contains the spinal cord
  • Thoracic cavity: Enclosed by the thoracic cage (rib cage); contains the heart, esophagus, trachea, lungs, and great vessels; separated from abdominal cavity by the diaphragm
  • Abdominal cavity: Enclosed by the abdominal wall; contains most digestive organs, kidneys, adrenal glands, pancreas, intestines, spleen
  • Pelvic cavity: Contains the urinary bladder, rectum, and internal reproductive organs

Structural Levels of the Body

  • Chemical level: atoms → molecules → macromolecules
  • Cellular level: cell — the basic unit of structure, function, and heredity; diameter 10–20 μm (lymphocyte 5 μm – nerve cell 1 mm)
  • Tissue level: groups of similarly differentiated cells with similar structure and function; 4 primary tissues = epithelial, connective, nervous, muscle tissue
  • Organ level: tissues combined appropriately to perform specific functions
    • Parenchymal organs: solid organs (liver, pancreas, kidney, thyroid gland)
    • Hollow organs: organs with empty interior (stomach, esophagus, intestine, bladder, uterus)
  • Organ system level: organs working together for a common purpose; 11 organ systems
  • Organism level: the complete individual

Organ Systems (11 Systems)

  • Integumentary: Skin, hair, nails, sweat glands, sebaceous glands → tissue protection, temperature regulation, sensory receptor support
  • Skeletal: Bones, ligaments, cartilage, tendons → structural framework, soft tissue protection, muscle attachment, blood cell production, mineral storage
  • Muscular: Skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, visceral muscle → movement, posture maintenance, heat generation
  • Nervous: Brain, spinal cord, nerves, sensory organs → detecting changes, receiving and interpreting sensory information, stimulating muscles and glands
  • Endocrine: Hormone-secreting glands (pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal, pancreas, ovaries, testes, pineal gland, thymus) → metabolic regulation
  • Cardiovascular: Heart, arteries, veins, capillaries, blood → blood movement through vessels and substance transport
  • Lymphatic: Lymph vessels, lymph nodes, thymus, spleen → tissue fluid return to blood, absorbed nutrient transport, body defense
  • Digestive: Mouth, tongue, teeth, salivary glands, pharynx, esophagus, stomach, liver, gallbladder, pancreas, small intestine, large intestine → food intake, breakdown, absorption
  • Respiratory: Nasal cavity, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, lungs → air passage, gas respiration, gas exchange
  • Urinary: Kidneys, ureters, urinary bladder, urethra → waste removal from blood, water and electrolyte balance
  • Reproductive: Male (scrotum, prostate, urethra, penis), Female (ovaries, uterine tubes, uterus, vagina) → gamete production, fetal development

Body Fluids

  • Total body water: approximately 60% of adult male body weight (adult female 51%, newborn 70%)
  • Intracellular fluid (ICF): Fluid inside cells; comprises approximately 2/3 of total body fluid
  • Extracellular fluid (ECF): Fluid outside cells; comprises remaining 1/3
    • Blood: fluid within blood vessels
    • Lymph: fluid within lymphatic vessels
    • Interstitial fluid (ISF) / Tissue fluid (TF): fluid between cells
  • Cells are surrounded by tissue fluid, absorbing O₂ and nutrients, and releasing metabolic products
  • Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻ are mostly abundant in extracellular fluid; K⁺, Mg²⁺, protein are mostly abundant in intracellular fluid
  • Protein is abundant in plasma but very scarce in interstitial fluid

3. Homeostasis

Concept of Internal Environment

  • Only skin and mucosal cells are in direct contact with the external air and water; other cells exist in extracellular fluid (body fluid)
  • Extracellular fluid is the living environment for nearly all cells, hence called the internal environment
  • Osmotic pressure, pH, electrolyte composition, gas composition, and body temperature must be maintained at optimal conditions

History of Homeostasis

  • Claude Bernard: 19th-century French physiologist who first proposed the concept of internal environment; "Life phenomena appear in diverse forms, but ultimately have the sole purpose of maintaining homeostasis of the internal environment"
  • Walter B. Cannon: Advanced Bernard's theory and coined the term homeostasis; derived from Greek Homeo (same) + stasis (staying); also called 'purposefulness'

Homeostatic Control Mechanisms

  • Receptor (sensor): Detects deviations from the set point
  • Integrating center: Usually the brain or spinal cord, but can also be endocrine gland cells; measures the relative intensity of sensory input
  • Effector: Responds by increasing or decreasing the activity of muscles or glands
  • Cell and organ functions are controlled by the nervous system and the endocrine system

Negative Feedback

  • Definition: When a substance or condition in the body increases (or rises) quantitatively, the increased factor acts directly or indirectly on the regulatory organ to decrease it, returning to the original state; and vice versa
  • Deviation from set point → receptor detection → transmission to integrating center → effector response (opposite direction) → return to original state
  • Homeostasis can be regarded as a state of dynamic constancy
  • Example: When body temperature exceeds the 37°C set point, brain receptors detect the deviation → promote sweat gland activity; when temperature drops → shivering raises body temperature

Positive Feedback

  • Definition: A phenomenon where the initial stimulus triggers a response that further amplifies the magnitude of the next stimulus; the effector's action amplifies the change that stimulated it
  • Ultimately, homeostasis must be maintained by negative feedback rather than positive feedback mechanisms
  • Examples: Blood coagulation (multi-stage positive feedback activation of clotting factors), urination (increased urination after initiation), labor contractions (uterine contractions become more frequent and stronger after initiation)
