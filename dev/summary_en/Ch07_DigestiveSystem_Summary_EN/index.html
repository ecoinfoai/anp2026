Chapter 07. Digestive System

1. Structure of Digestive Tract Wall

Overview of the Digestive System

  • Definition: System whose main function is to break down ingested food into smaller molecules and transport them into the blood
  • Digestion: Breaking complex, large food molecules into simple, small molecules
  • Absorption: Moving digested products into the blood
  • Digestive tube (alimentary tract): ~9m tube from mouth to anus → oral cavity, pharynx, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine
  • Digestive glands: Salivary glands, liver, pancreas

Four-Layer Structure of Digestive Tract Wall

  • Mucosa: Surface epithelium + lamina propria + muscularis mucosa → secretion of mucus/enzymes, defense, absorption
  • Submucosa: Glands, blood vessels, lymph vessels, nerves, loose connective tissue → nutrient supply, transport of absorbed materials
  • Muscular layer: Circular muscle + longitudinal muscle → movement of tract and contents (peristalsis, segmentation)
  • Serosa: Epithelial cells + connective tissue → serous fluid secretion, protection, lubrication

Nerve Supply of Digestive Tract

  • Parasympathetic nerves: Increase digestive system activity → via vagus nerve branches to esophagus, stomach, pancreas, gallbladder, small/large intestine
  • Sympathetic nerves: Opposite of parasympathetic → inhibit mixing/peristalsis, contract sphincters to inhibit material movement

2. Digestive Organs — Oral Cavity, Pharynx, Esophagus

Oral Cavity (mouth)

  • Functions: Receiving food, mastication, taste, phonation
  • Components: Upper/lower lips, cheeks, rima of mouth, oral angle
  • Palate: Anterior 2/3 = hard palate, posterior 1/3 = soft palate
  • Uvula: Central posterior extension of soft palate

Tongue

  • Terminal sulcus divides tongue into body + root + apex
  • Lingual papillae:
    • Filiform papillae: Most numerous, no taste buds
    • Fungiform papillae: Irregularly scattered, some taste buds
    • Circumvallate papillae: 7–12 large papillae anterior to terminal sulcus, abundant taste buds
    • Foliate papillae: Lateral surface of tongue root, present only until puberty then degenerate

Teeth

  • Structure: Crown + neck + root
  • Main component: Dentin → covered by enamel (hardest tissue in body) on crown, cementum on root
  • Hardness order: Enamel > dentin > cementum

Salivary Glands

  • Parotid gland: Largest gland, serous saliva with enzymes; mumps = viral inflammation
  • Submandibular gland: Mixed (serous + mucous) saliva
  • Sublingual gland: Thick mucous-rich saliva

Pharynx

  • Between oral cavity and esophagus, length ~12 cm
  • Pharyngeal tonsil; when hypertrophied = adenoid

Esophagus

  • Length ~25 cm, three narrowings (constrictions):
    • 1st: Esophageal beginning (13–15 cm from incisors)
    • 2nd: Tracheal bifurcation (22–25 cm from incisors)
    • 3rd: Diaphragmatic hiatus (38–40 cm from incisors)
  • Muscle composition: Upper 1/3 skeletal, middle 1/3 mixed, lower 1/3 smooth muscle

3. Stomach

Structure

  • Located in left upper abdomen, capacity 1.0–2.5 L
  • Regions: Cardia → fundus → body → pylorus
  • Pyloric sphincter: Regulates food discharge into duodenum
  • Greater curvature: Large lateral surface on left-inferior side
  • Lesser curvature: Short lateral surface on right-superior side
  • Angular notch: Common site for cancer and ulcers
  • Three muscle layers: Outer longitudinal + middle circular + inner oblique

Gastric Glands

  • Mucous neck cells: Secrete mucin → protect gastric wall
  • Chief cells: Secrete pepsinogen, rennin, lipase
  • Parietal cells: Largest gland cells; secrete HCl and endogastric (intrinsic) factor → deficiency causes pernicious anemia
  • Argentaffin cells: Presumed to secrete serotonin

4. Small Intestine

Overview

  • Diameter 2–3 cm, length 6–7 m → duodenum + jejunum + ileum
  • Function: Mixes bile, pancreatic juice, intestinal juice to complete digestion and absorb nutrients

Duodenum

  • Length 25 cm, C-shaped, no mesentery
  • 4 parts: Superior, descending, horizontal, ascending
  • Descending part contains major duodenal papilla → common bile duct + pancreatic duct open here

Jejunum and Ileum

  • Suspended from posterior abdominal wall by mesentery
  • Jejunum = proximal 2/5 (mainly left upper abdomen), ileum = distal 3/5 (right lower abdomen)

Wall Structure

  • Mucosa: Circular folds (plicae circularis) + intestinal villi → capillaries + central lacteal for nutrient absorption
  • Intestinal glands (Lieberkuhn glands): Between villi; Paneth cells at base
  • Submucosa: Brunner's glands (alkaline mucus) in duodenum; Peyer's patches in ileum
  • Muscular layer: Inner circular + outer longitudinal → segmentation and peristalsis

5. Large Intestine

Overview

  • Length ~1.5 m, main function: absorption of water and salts

Cecum

  • Widest part of digestive tract, length 7–8 cm
  • Vermiform appendix: Length 6–7 cm
  • Ileocecal valve: Prevents reflux
  • McBurney's point: Lateral 1/3 of line from umbilicus to right ASIS

Colon (4 segments)

  • Ascending colon: 20 cm, shortest segment of colon
  • Transverse colon: ~50 cm, longest segment of colon
  • Descending colon: ~25 cm
  • Sigmoid colon: ~45 cm, S-shaped

Rectum

  • Length ~10 cm
  • Rectal venous plexus congestion → hemorrhoids

Structural Features of Large Intestine

  • Taeniae coli: Three longitudinal muscle bands
  • Haustra: Pouched sacculations
  • No villi or circular folds in large intestine mucosa
  • Goblet cells: Mucus secretion for smooth defecation

6. Liver, Gallbladder, Pancreas

Liver

  • Largest gland in the body, weight ~1.5 kg
  • Lobes: Left + right (thicker and larger) + quadrate + caudate
  • Hepatic lobule: Basic structural unit; hepatic cords radiate from central vein
  • Kupffer cells (stellate cells): Phagocytic cells in sinusoidal endothelium
  • Bile pathway: Hepatocytes → common hepatic duct → (+ cystic duct) → common bile duct → major duodenal papilla
  • Sphincter of Oddi: At the opening of the major duodenal papilla

Gallbladder

  • Attached under right lobe of liver, capacity ~70 mL
  • Temporary storage of bile
  • Gallstones frequently occur

Pancreas

  • Posterior to stomach, weight ~70 g → head + body + tail
  • Exocrine: ~1,200 mL/day of digestive enzymes → trypsin (protein), amylase (carbohydrate), lipase (fat)
  • Endocrine: Pancreatic islets (islets of Langerhans), ~1 million
    • α cells → glucagon
    • β cells (75%) → insulin
    • δ cells → somatostatin

Peritoneum

  • Visceral peritoneum + parietal peritoneum → peritoneal cavity
  • Greater omentum: Hangs from greater curvature of stomach
  • Lesser omentum: Between liver and lesser curvature
  • Retroperitoneal organs: Duodenum, pancreas, kidneys, ascending/descending colon, etc.

7. Digestive Physiology — Oral and Gastric

Oral Digestion

  • Mastication: Mainly innervated by trigeminal nerve; lips/cheeks by facial nerve; tongue by lingual nerve
  • Saliva: Adults secrete 1.0–1.5 L/day
    • Ptyalin: Starch → dextrin + maltose
    • Mucin: Lubricates food for swallowing
    • Parotin: Secreted by parotid gland, promotes bone/tooth calcification
  • Functions of saliva: Digestion, swallowing aid, moisturizing/lubrication, cleansing, excretion, thermoregulation, fluid balance

Swallowing (Deglutition)

  • Swallowing center: Medulla oblongata
  • Phase 1 (oral): Voluntary movement
  • Phase 2 (pharyngeal): Involuntary reflex
  • Phase 3 (esophageal): Involuntary peristalsis → ~5 seconds from esophageal entrance to cardiac region

Gastric Digestion

  • Gastric motility: Peristaltic cycle ~20 seconds; parasympathetic promotes, sympathetic inhibits
  • Chyme: Semi-fluid mixture of gastric contents and gastric juice
  • Gastric emptying: Usually 2–3 hours after meal (normal completion 3–4 hours)
  • Enterogastric reflex: Inhibits gastric motility/secretion when duodenum is full

Gastric Juice

  • 1.0–1.5 L/day, pH 0.92–1.58 (strongly acidic)
  • Pepsin: Protein → proteoses, peptones
  • Lipase: Triglycerides → fatty acids + glycerol
  • Rennin: Caseinogen → casein (milk coagulation), abundant in infancy
  • HCl: Activates pepsinogen, maintains optimal pH, bactericidal, stimulates bile/pancreatic secretion

Three Phases of Gastric Secretion

  • Cephalic phase: 30–40% of total gastric juice
  • Gastric phase: 40–50% of total gastric juice
  • Intestinal phase: ~5% of total gastric juice

8. Digestive Physiology — Intestinal and Hepatic Functions

Small Intestine Digestion

  • Segmentation: Alternating contraction/relaxation → mixes food with digestive juices
  • Peristalsis: Wave-like contractions → moves food distally; 3–5 hours to reach large intestine
  • Pendular movement: Longitudinal muscle contraction/relaxation → mixing and transport

Pancreatic Juice

  • ~500–800 mL/day, pH 8.5 (slightly alkaline)
  • HCO₃⁻ ions → neutralize gastric acid
  • Trypsin/chymotrypsin: Protein → polypeptides (activated by enterokinase)
  • Lipase: Triglycerides → fatty acids + glycerol
  • Amylase: Starch → disaccharides / Maltase: Maltose → glucose / Lactase: Lactose → glucose + galactose
  • Secretion regulation: Secretin → NaHCO₃ secretion; CCK-pancreozymin → enzyme-rich pancreatic juice + bile secretion

Bile

  • 500–1,000 mL/day
  • Bile salts: Fat emulsification → facilitates lipase action, promotes fatty acid absorption
  • Bile pigments: Bilirubin → breakdown product of hemoglobin from RBC destruction
  • Other functions: Promotes absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), iron, calcium

Large Intestine Digestion

  • Large intestine fluid: Alkaline mucus, virtually no digestive enzymes
  • 4 hours post-meal → enters large intestine; 12 hours → reaches sigmoid colon
  • Mass peristalsis: Triggered by gastrocolic reflex, 2–3 times/day

Defecation

  • Internal anal sphincter: Reflexive relaxation (involuntary)
  • External anal sphincter: Voluntary muscle → can be contracted by will
  • Defecation center: Spinal cord; higher center: Cerebral cortex

Liver Functions

  • Carbohydrate metabolism (blood glucose maintenance), fat metabolism (phospholipid/cholesterol synthesis), protein metabolism (albumin/fibrinogen synthesis)
  • Ammonia → urea synthesis (ornithine cycle) → renal excretion
  • Drug/toxin detoxification, hormone inactivation
  • Heat production (major heat-producing organ)
  • Storage: Glycogen, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), iron, copper, vitamin B₁₂
  • Kupffer cells: Phagocytosis of bacteria/foreign bodies, immune antibody production
