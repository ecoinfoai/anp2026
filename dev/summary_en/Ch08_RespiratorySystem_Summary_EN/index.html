Chapter 08. Respiratory System

1. Structure and Function of Respiratory Organs

Definition of Respiration

  • Respiration: The entire process of inhaling oxygen from the outside, transporting it to cells, and exhaling CO₂ produced by cellular metabolism
  • External respiration: Gas exchange (O₂ in, CO₂ out) between pulmonary blood vessels and alveoli
  • Internal respiration: Gas exchange between capillary blood and tissue cells

Upper Airway — Nose

  • The nose is a triangular-conical projection at the center of the face
  • Nostrils (external nares) are the openings for air to enter the nasal cavity
  • Nasal hair (vibrissae) filters large airborne particles and dust

Upper Airway — Nasal Cavity

  • The nasal cavity is the hollow space behind the nose, divided left and right by the nasal septum
  • Separated from the cranial cavity by the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone; separated from oral cavity by the hard palate
  • Three nasal conchae (superior, middle, inferior) on the lateral wall create meatuses (superior, middle, inferior)
  • Conchae generate swirling airflow → centrifugal force deposits dust on nasal mucosa
  • Anterior floor: hard palate (bone-supported); posterior: soft palate (no bone)

Upper Airway — Paranasal Sinuses

  • Paranasal sinuses surround the nasal cavity in a ring-like pattern
  • Components: frontal sinus, sphenoid sinus, ethmoid sinus, maxillary sinus
  • Sphenoid sinus: inside sphenoid body above nasal cavity, opens into superior meatus
  • Ethmoid sinus: 3–15 small cavities in ethmoid bone, opens into superior and middle meatuses
  • Frontal sinus: paired cavities in frontal bone, opens via semilunar hiatus into middle meatus
  • Maxillary sinus: largest paranasal sinus in maxillary body; most prone to sinusitis

Upper Airway — Pharynx

  • Pharynx: ~13 cm funnel-shaped fibromuscular tube where digestive tract and airway intersect
  • Located behind nasal/oral cavities and larynx, anterior to cervical vertebrae
  • Air pathway: nasal cavity → nasopharynx → oropharynx → laryngopharynx → larynx

Lower Airway — Larynx

  • Larynx (voice box): routes air to trachea, food to esophagus, and aids phonation
  • Composed of 8 hyaline cartilages + 1 elastic cartilage (epiglottis); located at C4–C6 level
  • Laryngeal cavity: divided into 3 parts by vestibular folds and vocal folds
  • Glottis: space between left and right vocal folds
  • Phonation: vocal muscles narrow the glottis; forced air vibrates to produce sound
  • Vestibular folds (false vocal cords): not directly involved in phonation; add moisture to air
  • Sex differences: male vocal cords 13 mm, female 10 mm; males have laryngeal prominence (Adam's apple)
  • Laryngeal cartilages: 3 large (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottic) + 3 pairs of small (arytenoid, cuneiform, corniculate)
    • Thyroid cartilage: largest laryngeal cartilage, shield-shaped
    • Cricoid cartilage: ring-shaped, connects thyroid cartilage above to first tracheal ring below
    • Epiglottic cartilage: tennis racket-shaped; covers laryngeal inlet during swallowing
    • Arytenoid cartilage: pyramid-shaped, adjusts vocal cord position and tension for phonation

Lower Airway — Trachea

  • Trachea: ~2.5 cm diameter, ~10–12 cm length, from larynx to tracheal bifurcation
  • Descends from C7 to T5, anterior to esophagus
  • Lined with ciliated mucosa that moves mucus from lungs toward pharynx
  • Composed of 15–20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings
    • Open side: allows esophageal expansion during swallowing
    • Rigid side: maintains airway patency during pressure changes

Lower Airway — Bronchi

  • At the tracheal bifurcation, left and right primary bronchi form
  • Primary bronchi: right is shorter, wider, more vertical than left → foreign objects more likely to enter right side
  • Secondary bronchi (lobar): right 3, left 2 — matches number of lung lobes
  • Tertiary bronchi (segmental): right ~10, left ~8
  • Bronchioles: diameter <1 mm
  • Terminal bronchioles: conducting airway, 50–80 per lobule
  • Respiratory bronchioles: ~0.5 mm diameter, have alveoli attached → participate in gas exchange
  • Alveolar ducts: 2–10 small tubes from respiratory bronchioles
  • Alveolar sacs: thin-walled pouch structures

Lungs

  • Lungs: paired semi-conical solid organs filling the thoracic cavity
  • Volume: right 1,200 cc, left 1,000 cc; weight: right 600 g, left 500 g (ratio 10:9)
  • Covered by pleura
  • Apex: extends 2–3 cm above clavicle
  • Base: rests on diaphragm
  • Hilum: entry/exit point for bronchi, pulmonary arteries, bronchial vessels, lymphatics, nerves
  • Right lung: 3 lobes (upper, middle, lower) — oblique fissure + horizontal fissure
  • Left lung: 2 lobes (upper, lower) — oblique fissure

Alveoli

  • Alveoli: thin-walled air sacs, ~300–500 million in number
  • Gas exchange surface area: ~70–100 m²
  • Type I pneumocytes: cover 95% of alveolar surface, thin and broad for gas exchange
  • Type II pneumocytes (septal cells): secrete surfactant to prevent alveolar collapse during expiration
  • Dust cells (alveolar macrophages): phagocytize dust and foreign particles

Functions of the Lungs

  • ① Secrete surfactant
  • ② Secrete kinin (vasodilation)
  • ③ Secrete gastrin (stomach hormone)
  • ④ Convert angiotensin I → angiotensin II
  • ⑤ Synthesize, store, release prostaglandin; involved in insulin metabolism — all via pulmonary capillary endothelium

Mediastinum

  • Mediastinum: central thoracic space between left and right lungs
  • Boundaries: mediastinal pleura (lateral), sternum (anterior), vertebral column (posterior), diaphragm (inferior)
  • Superior: trachea, esophagus, thymus, aorta, superior vena cava
  • Anterior: thymus, internal thoracic artery/vein
  • Middle: heart
  • Posterior: esophagus, vagus nerve, aorta, thoracic duct

2. Respiratory Physiology

Respiratory Mechanics — Inspiration

  • Inspiration is an active process requiring muscle contraction and energy expenditure
  • Diaphragm contraction: phrenic nerve (C₃–C₅) stimulation → diaphragm descends → intrathoracic pressure drops to -2.5 to -6 mmHg → alveoli expand, volume increases ~250–300 ml. Diaphragmatic breathing = abdominal breathing (more common in males)
  • External intercostal muscle contraction: ribs elevate → thorax expands → intrathoracic pressure drops → alveoli expand. External intercostals + erector spinae = thoracic breathing
  • Abdominal muscle relaxation: assists diaphragm descent
  • Surfactant reduces surface tension: surfactant (lipoprotein) reduces alveolar surface tension → facilitates expansion

Respiratory Mechanics — Expiration

  • Expiration is a passive process (reverse of inspiration), requires no energy
  • Primary muscle: internal intercostal muscle

Lung Volumes and Capacities

  • Tidal volume (TV): air inhaled/exhaled per normal breath = 500 ml
  • Inspiratory reserve volume (IRV): additional air that can be inhaled after normal inspiration = 3,100 ml
  • Expiratory reserve volume (ERV): additional air that can be exhaled after normal expiration = 1,200 ml
  • Residual volume (RV): air remaining after maximal expiration = 1,200 ml (20% of TLC; ≥35% suggests dysfunction)
  • Inspiratory capacity (IC) = IRV + TV = 3,600 ml
  • Functional residual capacity (FRC) = RV + ERV = 2,400 ml
  • Vital capacity (VC) = IC + ERV = 4,800 ml (decreases significantly after age 40)
  • Total lung capacity (TLC) = VC + RV = 6,000 ml
  • Dead space: ~150 ml of air that stays in conducting airways without reaching alveoli

Alveolar Gas Exchange

  • Atmospheric composition: N₂ 78.97%, O₂ 21%, CO₂ 0.03%
  • At 760 mmHg: N₂ 600, O₂ 159, CO₂ 0.3 mmHg
  • Alveolar air: O₂ ~100, CO₂ ~40, water vapor 47, N₂ 573 mmHg
  • Arterial blood: O₂ ~95 (some sources 100), CO₂ ~40 mmHg
  • Venous blood (pulmonary): O₂ ~40, CO₂ ~46 mmHg

Oxygen Transport by Blood

  • Most O₂ is chemically bound to hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin); only ~1% is physically dissolved
  • Oxygen saturation increases as O₂ partial pressure rises, approaching 100%
  • Oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve: shifts right when CO₂ partial pressure increases → at same O₂ level, higher CO₂ means lower O₂ saturation

Carbon Dioxide Transport by Blood

  • Most CO₂ transport is chemical (physical dissolution is minimal)
  • ~80%: as bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) in plasma
  • ~20%: as carbaminohemoglobin (bound to hemoglobin)

Neural Control of Respiration

  • Respiratory center: spans from medulla oblongata to pons
    • Medulla: controls respiratory rhythm
    • Pons: controls respiratory rate
  • Hering-Breuer reflex: vagus nerve-mediated reflex that provides rhythmicity to respiration
  • Cerebral cortex can override automatic mechanisms to some extent (e.g., singing, blowing a balloon)

Chemical Control of Respiration

  • Blood CO₂ affects excitability of respiratory center
  • Peripheral chemoreceptors: carotid body (at internal carotid bifurcation) and aortic body (at aortic arch) — detect ↑CO₂ or ↓O₂ → increase respiratory center excitability → more vigorous breathing
  • Respiratory center itself is directly excited by CO₂ → increased CO₂ deepens and accelerates breathing

Abnormal Breathing Patterns

  • Tachypnea: increased respiratory rate
  • Dyspnea: difficult/labored breathing — causes include airway resistance (asthma), fluid accumulation (peritonitis, pleuritis), impaired gas exchange (pneumonia)
  • Cheyne-Stokes respiration: cyclical pattern of weak/absent breathing → gradually increasing → gradually decreasing → apnea — seen in CNS disorders, heart failure, uremia, narcotic poisoning, near death
  • Kussmaul respiration: deep, slow continuous breathing — seen in severe metabolic acidosis (diabetes, renal failure) with coma
  • Biot respiration: irregular deep breaths interspersed with apnea/shallow breathing — seen in intracranial hypertension with encephalitis, meningitis, brain tumors
