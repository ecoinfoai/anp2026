Chapter 11. Urinary System

1. Structure of the Urinary System

Overview

  • Components: Kidney, ureter, urethra, urinary bladder
  • Functions:
    • Excretion of metabolic waste via urine
    • Blood pressure regulation by adjusting urine output
    • Electrolyte balance maintenance
    • Acid-base balance regulation via H⁺ excretion
    • Blood pressure regulation via renin release
    • Red blood cell production regulation via erythropoietin secretion

Renin

  • Definition: A proteolytic enzyme secreted by juxtaglomerular apparatus (JGA) cells in the afferent arteriole wall
  • Mechanism: α2-globulin → Angiotensin I (10-amino acid peptide) → hydrolysis → Angiotensin II → peripheral vasoconstriction → blood pressure increase
  • Potency of Angiotensin II: 40–50 times that of noradrenaline
  • Secretion stimulus: Decreased arterial blood pressure entering the kidney

External Structure of the Kidney

  • Location: Upper posterior abdominal wall, bilateral retroperitoneal organs flanking the vertebral column
  • Height: Upper pole at T12, lower pole at L3
  • Asymmetry: Right kidney slightly lower than left
  • Morphology: Bean-shaped solid organ, ~10 cm long, ~130 g
  • Hilum: Medial concavity; entry/exit for blood vessels, lymphatics, nerves, and ureter

Internal Structure of the Kidney

  • Cortex: Outer layer, reddish-brown
  • Medulla: Inner layer, red
  • Renal columns: Cortical tissue extending inward
  • Renal pyramids: ~10 pyramids separated by renal columns
  • Pathway: Renal papilla → renal calices → renal pelvis → ureter

Nephron

  • Definition: Functional unit of the kidney; ~1 million per kidney
  • Components: Renal corpuscle + renal tubule
  • Types: Cortical nephron, juxtamedullary nephron

Renal Corpuscle

  • Location: Within the cortex, ~0.2 mm in diameter
  • Components: Glomerulus + glomerular capsule (Bowman's capsule)
  • Glomerulus: Afferent arteriole → capillary tuft → efferent arteriole
  • Glomerular capsule: Double-layered epithelial membrane; filtrate collects in the capsular space

Renal Tubule Pathway

  • Glomerular capsule → proximal convoluted tubule → loop of Henle → distal convoluted tubule → collecting duct → renal papilla → renal calyx → renal pelvis

Renal Blood Supply

  • Blood flow: ~1,000–1,200 mL/min (both kidneys), approximately 1/4 of cardiac output
  • Pathway: Renal artery → interlobar artery → arcuate artery → interlobular artery → afferent arteriole → glomerulus → efferent arteriole → peritubular capillary network → interlobular vein → arcuate vein → interlobar vein → renal vein
  • Vasa recta: Branches of efferent arterioles supplying the loop of Henle and collecting ducts

2. Ureter, Bladder, and Urethra

Ureter

  • Length: 25–28 cm
  • Wall layers: Mucosa, muscular layer, adventitia
  • Muscular layer: Inner longitudinal, outer circular (opposite of GI tract); additional outer longitudinal layer in the lower portion
  • Peristalsis: 2–4 times per minute

Urinary Bladder

  • Location: Behind the pubic symphysis in the pelvic cavity
  • Capacity: 500–700 mL
  • Vesical trigone: Triangular area connecting two ureteral orifices and internal urethral orifice
  • Wall structure: Transitional epithelium mucosa + muscular layer (inner longitudinal, middle circular, outer longitudinal) + adventitia
  • Detrusor muscle: Collective term for the bladder muscular layers

Urethra

  • Male urethra: ~20 cm; serves both urinary and reproductive functions
  • Female urethra: 3–4 cm; external urethral orifice located anterior to the vaginal opening and posterior to the clitoris
  • Female infection risk: Short urethra close to anus → E. coli migration → urethritis → cystitis → pyelonephritis

3. Physiology of the Urinary System

Urine Formation Process

  • Three processes: Glomerular filtration → tubular reabsorption → tubular secretion

Urine Characteristics

  • pH: ~5.0–7.0 (meat diet=acidic, vegetable diet=alkaline)
  • Specific gravity: 1.015–1.025 (inversely proportional to urine volume)
  • Daily volume: 1–1.8 L
  • Color: Urochrome
  • Solid components (~60 g/day): Inorganic 25 g (60% NaCl) + organic 35 g
  • Organic: Urea predominates (90% of urinary nitrogen); remaining 10% = uric acid, creatinine, ammonia, hippuric acid, amino acids

Glomerular Filtration

  • Principle: Pressure-driven filtration; all plasma components except proteins pass into Bowman's capsule
  • Filtration pressure: Blood pressure (70 mmHg) − plasma colloidal osmotic pressure (25 mmHg) − Bowman's space pressure (10 mmHg) = 35 mmHg
  • GFR: Average 125 mL/min in resting adult males
  • Renal plasma flow: Average 660 mL/min
  • Renal blood flow: 1,200 mL/min

Tubular Reabsorption

  • Reabsorption rate: >99% of filtrate
  • Primary site: Proximal convoluted tubule to straight tubule
  • Reabsorbed substances: Water, glucose, amino acids, Na⁺, Cl⁻
  • Hormonal regulation (distal convoluted tubule):
    • ADH (antidiuretic hormone): Promotes water reabsorption
    • Aldosterone: Promotes Na⁺ reabsorption, increases K⁺ excretion, HCO₃⁻ reabsorption → urine acidification

Tubular Secretion

  • Secreted substances: Creatinine, ammonia, hippuric acid, K⁺, HPO₄⁻
  • Foreign substance excretion: PAH, phenol red, penicillin → not reabsorbed, actively secreted

Clearance

  • Definition: Volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per unit time
  • Formula: C (mL/min) = UV/P
  • Inulin and mannitol: Filtered but neither reabsorbed nor secreted → clearance = GFR

Urination (Micturition)

  • Urge onset: Bladder volume 200–300 mL → sudden pressure rise → urge to urinate
  • Intravesical pressure during urination: ~30 cmH₂O
  • Micturition reflex: Primary center in the sacral spinal cord (autonomic reflex)
  • Neural control: Sympathetic nervous system uninvolved; parasympathetic promotes micturition
  • Higher centers: Brain can consciously inhibit or initiate micturition reflex

Acid-Base Balance Regulation

  • Body fluid pH: 7.35–7.45
  • Acidosis: pH below normal; Alkalosis: pH above normal
  • Buffer systems: Bicarbonate, phosphate, protein (three levels)
  • Kidney role: Exchange H⁺ for Na⁺ in tubular epithelium; ammonia (NH₃) production

Osmotic Pressure Regulation

  • Insensible evaporation: ~800 mL/day from skin
  • Obligatory urine: 500 mL/day
  • Adjustable urine volume: 900 mL/day
  • Water excess → edema; Water deficit → dehydration
  • Dehydration response: Osmolality ↑ → thirst → ↑ water intake + ↑ ADH secretion → concentrated urine
  • Osmolality decrease: ADH secretion inhibited
  • Na⁺ regulation: Aldosterone involvement
