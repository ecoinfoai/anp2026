Chapter 03. Skin

Overview

  • Skin: A tough membrane covering the entire body surface that provides body protection, temperature regulation, metabolism, and fat storage
  • Surface area: 1.6–1.8 m² in adults; weight approximately 4.0 kg
  • Functions: ① Body protection ② Temperature regulation ③ Excretion of water, lipids, etc. ④ Detection of touch, thermal sensations, and pain ⑤ Absorption of drugs ⑥ Vitamin D storage
  • Skin color: Determined by pigments (keratohyalin, melanin) and capillary distribution
  • Mongolian spot: Blue spots on newborns' backs (especially buttocks); disappear with growth

1. Structure of the Skin

Layer Structure

  • Skin consists of epidermis and dermis, with subcutaneous tissue beneath
  • Thickness: average 1–4 mm
  • Thick on palms and soles; thin on eyelids, ear pinnae, penis, scrotum

1) Epidermis

  • Outermost layer; stratified squamous epithelium
  • Thickness: palms/soles 0.8–1.4 mm; other areas 0.07–0.12 mm
  • Layers (inside to outside): Germinative layer (basal + spinous) → Granular layer → Clear layer → Cornified layer
  • Epidermis renews every 25–45 days
  • Epidermis has no blood vessels

(1) Cornified Layer (Stratum Corneum)

  • Dead squamous epithelial cells; keratinized thick layer; no nuclei; filled with keratin
  • Keratin: exceptionally tough fibrous protein; prevents moisture loss; resists biological, physical, and chemical attacks
  • Continuously shed and replaced by basal layer cell division

(2) Clear Layer (Stratum Lucidum)

  • Thin transparent layer below cornified layer
  • Contains eleidin, a semi-fluid substance

(3) Granular Layer (Stratum Granulosum)

  • 3–5 layers of spindle-shaped cells below clear layer
  • Nuclei clearly visible (unlike cornified and clear layers)
  • Contains irregular granules of keratohyalin

(4) Germinative Layer

  • Deepest layer of epidermis; divided into 2 sublayers
  • Spinous layer (stratum spinosum): Polygonal cells with short spiny projections forming intercellular bridges
  • Basal layer (stratum basale): Single-layered columnar epithelium; continuously divides producing millions of cells daily; contains melanin pigment granules determining skin color; closest to dermis; receives nutrients via diffusion from dermis

2) Dermis

  • Connective tissue layer below epidermis; rich in elastic fibers, blood vessels, and nerves
  • Thickness: approximately 0.3–3 mm
  • Divided into vascular papilla and nervous papilla
  • Rich blood supply maintains body temperature homeostasis
  • Divided into papillary layer and reticular layer

(1) Papillary Layer

  • Surface layer of dermis; forms ridges and grooves on skin surface
  • Dermal papillae: finger-like projections protruding into epidermis
  • Contains capillary loops supplying oxygen and nutrients to epidermis
  • Contains pain receptors (free nerve endings) and Meissner's corpuscles (touch receptors)
  • On palms and soles: papillae arranged in clear patterns → increases friction → fingerprints
  • Papillary patterns are genetically determined

(2) Reticular Layer

  • Deepest skin layer
  • Composed of collagen fibers and elastic fibers
  • Contains blood vessels, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and Pacinian corpuscles (deep pressure receptors)
  • Macrophages prevent bacterial invasion
  • Aging reduces collagen and elastic fibers → wrinkles
  • Langer's lines: Incisions parallel to these lines produce smaller wounds; perpendicular incisions produce larger wounds

3) Subcutaneous Tissue

  • Loose connective tissue binding skin to underlying fascia
  • Well-developed blood vessels and lymphatic vessels in network pattern
  • Rich in fat cells → also called subcutaneous fat layer
  • Fat tissue acts as heat insulator for temperature regulation
  • Well-developed in abdomen and buttocks (≥3 cm thick); more developed in females
  • Absent in eyelids, penis, scrotum, ear pinnae

4) Nerve Endings of the Skin

  • Free nerve ending: pain; Meissner's corpuscle: touch; Ruffini body: warmth; Krause's end-bulb: cold; Pacinian corpuscle: pressure
  • Muscle spindle and Golgi tendon organ: proprioceptors for deep sensation

5) Blood Vessels of the Skin

  • Supply nutrients and regulate body temperature
  • Arteriovenous anastomoses present
  • Blood flow can vary up to 100-fold: 20–50 mL/min (constricted) to 2–5 L/min (fully dilated)
  • Vascular regulation occurs reflexively via sympathetic nerves

2. Skin Appendages

  • Include: skin glands (sweat glands, sebaceous glands, mammary glands), hair and follicles, nails
  • All appendages originate from the dermis

1) Hair

  • Tough keratinized product of epidermis on body surface
  • Functions: protection, temperature preservation, touch sensation
  • Hair shaft (above skin); Hair root (within skin)
  • Color determined by melanin content
  • Hair grows from hair papilla; head hair grows approximately 1.5–2.0 cm per month

2) Sebaceous Glands

  • Located in dermis continuous with hair follicles; monoalveolar glands
  • Sebum contains fat, cholesterol, albumin, salts; prevents hair drying and excessive moisture evaporation
  • Arrector pili muscle (piloerector muscle): Contraction causes goose skin; absent in eyebrows, eyelashes, ear hair, armpit hair
  • Arrector pili muscle innervated by sympathetic nerves

3) Nails

  • Keratinized epidermis of dorsal fingers and toes; protective function
  • Nail body and nail root; matrix unguis responsible for nail growth
  • Nails appear pink due to rich blood supply in underlying dermis

4) Sweat Glands

  • Over 2.5 million per person
  • Eccrine glands (small sweat glands): Distributed throughout body; coiled tubular glands
    • Sweat: clear secretion containing water, NaCl, vitamin C, metabolic waste (ammonia, urea, uric acid), lactic acid
    • Acidic (pH 4–6); inhibits bacterial growth
    • Important for thermoregulation and excretion
  • Apocrine glands (large sweat glands): Found only in axillae, nipples, eyelids, external auditory canal, genital area, perianal area
    • Larger than eccrine glands; ducts open into hair follicles
    • Secretion: milky or yellowish; contains fatty acids and proteins
    • Activated at puberty by male hormones; minimal role in thermoregulation
    • Cause of osmidrosis (body odor)

5) Mammary Glands

  • Modified sweat glands in adult female breast
  • Located above pectoralis major fascia, at 2nd–6th(7th) rib level; left breast slightly larger
  • Nipple has 12–15 openings of lactiferous ducts
  • Areola darkens during pregnancy/postpartum
  • Internal structure: 15–20 lactiferous lobes arranged radially → lactiferous ducts → lactiferous sinuses → nipple openings
  • Breast weight: 150–200 g; during lactation 400–500 g
  • Breast size determined by fat amount, not glandular tissue → size and milk production are largely unrelated

3. Burns

  • Burns: skin damage from heat, hot liquids, steam, chemicals, electricity, UV radiation
  • Classified into 3 degrees by damage severity

Burn Degrees

  • 1st degree: Epidermis only; redness; no scarring; heals in 2–3 days; includes sunburn
  • 2nd degree: Epidermis and dermis; pain and blisters; epithelial regeneration possible; no permanent scarring if infection prevented
  • 3rd degree: Through epidermis, dermis, subcutaneous fat, and fascia; glands and follicles cannot regenerate; severe scarring; requires skin graft surgery

Burn Complications

  • Fluid loss (protein and electrolyte-containing) through burned surfaces
  • Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, renal failure, circulatory shock possible
  • Rule of nines: Body divided into 11 regions (each 9% of body surface area); remaining 1% is genital/perineal area
  • Severe burn criteria: >25% 2nd degree, >10% 3rd degree, 3rd degree on face or hands/feet

4. Body Temperature

Thermoregulation Animals

  • Poikilothermal (cold-blooded): Body temperature varies with environment
  • Homeothermal (warm-blooded): Body temperature relatively constant

Human Body Temperature

  • Varies by body region; extremities fluctuate with environmental temperature
  • Core temperature is nearly constant
  • Oral measurement: 37.0°C; Rectal measurement: 0.6°C higher than oral
  • Lowest at 6–7 AM, highest at 6–7 PM (difference ~1°C)
  • Children approximately 1°C higher than adults
  • Temperature maintained when heat production = heat loss

1) Heat Production

  • Major heat-producing organs: liver, heart, brain, and all endocrine glands → approximately 50% of body heat
  • Skeletal muscles (comprising ~1/2 of body volume) → approximately 40% of heat

2) Heat Loss

  • Occurs through radiation, conduction, and evaporation
  • Radiation: Infrared exchange between body and surrounding objects; ~60% of heat loss at 21°C nude
  • Conduction: Heat transfer to objects in contact; ~3% to solid objects, ~12% to air
  • Convection: Heated air rises, replaced by cooler air; essentially a result of conduction
  • Evaporation: Water changing to gas state; 0.58 kcal lost per gram of water evaporated; ~25% of heat loss; daily evaporation ~600 mL
  • As ambient temperature approaches body temperature, evaporation proportion increases (75% at 31°C)

3) Thermoregulation

  • Central control: hypothalamus
  • When temperature rises: anterior hypothalamus neurons excited → skin vasodilation, increased sweating, decreased muscle tone → increased heat loss, decreased heat production
  • When temperature falls: skin thermoreceptors → posterior hypothalamus neurons excited → skin vasoconstriction, decreased sweating, increased skeletal muscle tone, shivering → decreased heat loss, increased heat production
  • Hypothalamus functions like a thermostat set at 37°C in healthy individuals

4) Fever

  • Core temperature (rectal) elevated above 38°C
  • Caused by exogenous pyrogens (bacterial infection, allergies, cancer) raising the thermoregulatory set point; thermoregulation mechanism itself is intact
  • Also caused by mechanical stimuli (cerebral hemorrhage, brain tumor, skull base fracture) and pyrogens
  • Defervescence: When cause is removed, set point returns to normal → vasodilation and sweating → heat loss promoted
  • Antipyretics: Aspirin inhibits prostaglandin synthesis → reduces fever
