Chapter 04. Blood

1. Blood

Composition of Blood

  • Definition: Blood has pH 7.35–7.45, specific gravity 1,050–1,060, slightly alkaline; color varies from bright red to dark red depending on oxygen content
  • Volume: About 8–9% of body weight in adults, averaging 5–6 L
  • Viscosity: About 2× higher than water due to cellular components
  • Components:
    • Blood cells (45%): RBC, WBC, platelets
    • Plasma (55%): pale yellow fluid, ~90% water, remainder mostly plasma proteins
  • Hematocrit (HCT): Volume ratio of RBCs in blood, normal 42–45%
  • RBCs and platelets lack nuclei; WBCs have nuclei
  • Plasma proteins: Albumin, globulin, fibrinogen

Functions of Blood

  • Respiratory gas transport: O₂ from alveoli to tissues; CO₂ from tissues to lungs
  • Nutrient transport: Absorbed nutrients from intestines to body tissues
  • Waste transport: Urea, uric acid, lactic acid, creatinine → excretory organs (kidneys)
  • Body defense: Immune substances, phagocytosis against bacteria
  • Electrolyte and water regulation: Maintains blood pH at ~7.4
  • Hormone transport: Hormones from endocrine glands to target organs
  • Blood coagulation: Prevents blood loss from hemorrhage
  • Temperature regulation: Maintains constant temperature of skeletal muscles and liver

2. Blood Cell Formation and Destruction

Hematopoiesis

  • Origin: All blood cells originate from hemocytoblasts (stem cells) in bone marrow
  • Fetal hematopoiesis stages: Megaloblastic phase → Hepatic phase → Myeloid phase

Postnatal Blood Cell Formation

  • Hematopoietic organs: Myeloid tissue and lymphoid tissue
  • Until day 15 after birth: Liver and spleen produce RBCs and WBCs
  • After age 3: Red bone marrow gradually replaced by yellow bone marrow
    • Red bone marrow: Active hematopoiesis
    • Yellow bone marrow: Filled with fat, minimal hematopoietic function
  • Major hematopoietic sites: Sternum, vertebrae, clavicle, ribs, ilium
  • Hematopoietic longevity: Flat bones (vertebrae, sternum, skull, scapula, pelvis) maintain function longer than long bones (humerus, femur)
  • Required for RBC maturation: Vitamin B₁₂, folic acid, iron (Fe), copper (Cu)

Blood Cell Destruction

  • RBCs destroyed by macrophage system in liver, spleen, and bone marrow
  • RBC lifespan: ~120 days
  • Hemoglobin (Hb) → heme + iron + globin → recycled for hemoglobin synthesis
  • Erythropoietin: Produced by kidneys; stimulates RBC production in response to low oxygen

3. Formed Elements

Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells)

  • Shape: Biconcave disc, diameter 7.2 μm, edge thickness 2.2 μm, center 1 μm, surface area 120 μm², volume 87 μm²
  • Mature cells lack nuclei and do not undergo cell division
  • Count: Males 4.2–6.2 million/mm³, Females 4.5–5.1 million/mm³
  • Hemoglobin: 1 globin + 4 heme molecules, MW 68,000
    • 1 g hemoglobin binds 1.34 mL O₂
    • Binds O₂ within 0.01 sec at high PO₂; releases O₂ at low PO₂
    • CO affinity ~200× greater than O₂
  • Oxyhemoglobin (HbO₂): Hemoglobin bound to O₂
  • Reduced hemoglobin (HbCO₂): Hemoglobin without O₂
  • Oxygen dissociation curve: Hemoglobin-O₂ binding vs. PO₂

Anemia

  • Decreased RBC count or hemoglobin → tissue hypoxia → pallor, fatigue
  • Types: Aplastic anemia, hemolytic anemia, iron-deficiency anemia, pernicious anemia, sickle cell anemia, thalassemia

Jaundice

  • Abnormally elevated bilirubin → yellow discoloration of skin and mucous membranes
  • Hemolytic jaundice: Mass RBC destruction → excessive bilirubin
  • Hepatic jaundice: Liver disease prevents bilirubin excretion via bile ducts
  • Obstructive jaundice: Gallstones or tumors block bilirubin passage to small intestine
  • Neonatal jaundice: Appears 2–3 days after birth, resolves within ~10 days

Leukocytes (White Blood Cells)

  • 5,000–10,000/mm³, average 7,400; no sex difference
  • Granulocytes:
    • Neutrophils: 12–14 μm, 60–70%, phagocytosis (acute inflammation), most important in innate immunity
    • Eosinophils: 12 μm, 1–4%, increase in allergic reactions and parasitic infections
    • Basophils: 9 μm, ~1%, contain heparin, prevent coagulation, promote healing at inflammation sites
  • Agranulocytes:
    • Lymphocytes: 9–14 μm, 20–30%, produced in lymph nodes/thymus/spleen, produce antibodies
    • Monocytes: 15–20 μm, 2–6%, produced in bone marrow, develop into macrophages, phagocytosis (chronic inflammation)

Platelets (Thrombocytes)

  • Non-nucleated, granular, colorless, 2–4 μm diameter, irregular disc shape
  • Count: 130,000–360,000/mm³, lifespan ~1 week
  • Derived from megakaryocyte cytoplasm fragments
  • Secreted substances: Serotonin, histamine, ATPase, Ca²⁺, K⁺, thromboplastin

Blood Coagulation

  • Step 1: Clotting factors released from platelets and damaged cells
  • Step 2: Inactive proteins → prothrombin activator
  • Step 3: Prothrombin → thrombin
  • Step 4: Thrombin converts fibrinogen → fibrin
  • Step 5: Fibrin mesh traps RBCs and platelets → clot formation
  • Hemophilia: Congenital deficiency of clotting factor VIII
  • Vitamin K: Required for prothrombin synthesis in liver
  • Warfarin: Vitamin K antagonist → treats thrombosis

Plasma

  • 55% of blood volume, 90% water, 10% solutes
  • Plasma proteins (7–8% of plasma):
    • Albumin: Smallest MW, 60%, synthesized in liver, regulates colloid osmotic pressure; deficiency → edema
    • Globulins: 36%; alpha and beta (liver), gamma (lymphoid tissue) → gamma globulin = immunoglobulin (Ig)
    • Fibrinogen: ~4%, key role in blood coagulation

Blood Types

  • ABO system: Agglutinogens (A, B) on RBC membrane + agglutinins (anti-A, anti-B) in serum → 4 types: A, B, AB, O
  • Transfusion: Loss of >30–40% blood volume can be fatal; efficacy depends on timing, rate, volume, fluid type
  • Inheritance: Blood type inherited from parents, unchanged throughout life

Rh Factor

  • Most Koreans are Rh-positive; only 0.4% Rh-negative
  • Repeated Rh-positive transfusion to Rh-negative person → agglutinin formation → transfusion reaction
  • Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive fetus → possible stillbirth
  • Rh factor is dominant inheritance

4. Immunity

Definition of Immunity

  • Defense mechanism protecting host from infections
  • Antigen: Substance that induces immune response
  • Antibody: Binds specifically to antigen, neutralizes toxins and bacteria

Cellular Immunity

  • WBCs phagocytize and digest microorganisms
  • T-lymphocytes: ~70–80% of lymphocytes
    • Killer T-cells (Tc), Helper T-cells (Th), Suppressor T-cells, Cytotoxic T-cells
  • Cytokines: Polypeptides secreted by T-cells; specifically lymphokines
  • Primary cause of transplant rejection (exception: identical twins)
  • Immunosuppressants → temporary graft adaptation
  • Autoimmune reaction: Immune system attacks own normal cells → autoimmune disease

Humoral Immunity

  • B-lymphocytes produce antibodies with Helper T-cell assistance
  • B-cell clones → plasma cells or memory cells
  • Primary response: Plasma cells produce antibodies
  • Secondary response: Memory B-cells rapidly differentiate into plasma cells for massive antibody production

Immunoglobulins (Ig)

  • Gamma globulins produced by plasma cells in reticuloendothelial system
  • IgG: MW 150,000–160,000, most abundant, only Ig to cross placenta, innate immunity
  • IgA: MW 180,000, found in tears/nasal secretions/bronchial mucosa/intestinal mucosa/milk, bacterial defense
  • IgM: MW 900,000, largest antibody, first to appear at inflammation sites, activates macrophages, includes anti-A and anti-B
  • IgD: MW 150,000, antigen receptor, childhood milk allergy
  • IgE: MW 200,000, parasitic infections/hay fever/allergic reactions

Allergy

  • Abnormal reaction to specific substance due to sensitivity
  • Allergens: Mainly proteins (eggs, milk, fish, meat, grains)
  • Symptoms: Hives, rash, indigestion, diarrhea, asthma, hay fever
  • Mechanism: IgE → binds mast cells → histamine release → vasodilation, swelling, mucus secretion, smooth muscle contraction

Active and Passive Immunization

  • Immunity: Innate + Acquired (active, passive)
  • Active immunization: Via vaccination; resistance develops in 5–14 days, lasts years
  • Passive immunization: Receiving pre-formed antibodies; immediate effect, lasts days to weeks; mother-to-fetus/infant transfer possible
